In February I published a post on my blog titled “You Have at Most 12 Months to Adapt to the AI Era.”

After I hit publish, it felt too strong, so I left it on the blog and did not push it widely.

Looking back today, that article was actually too tame.

Twelve months?

Maybe six. Or even less.

---

## What Is Happening Right Now

In March 2026, BYD released its 2025 annual report.

Eight hundred billion in revenue, 4.6 million vehicles sold worldwide, four years running at the top—the headline numbers look great.

In a corner of the filing, though, sits a number that stops you cold.

In a single year, nearly a hundred thousand people vanished from BYD.

Not because cars stopped selling.

The roles that were cut were almost all frontline production work that could be automated. Core technical headcount did not shrink—it actually grew by more than four thousand.

Profits were up and headcount went down not because the company was broken, but because on the line, machines had already surpassed people at the repetitive motions.

Meanwhile, the big tech names were hardly quiet.

Since May 2025, Microsoft has cut more than fifteen thousand roles, hitting Azure, global sales, and engineering, among others. Meta eliminated over a thousand positions in Reality Labs. Amazon, across two rounds, shed roughly thirty thousand jobs—the largest “trim” in its thirty-one-year history.

Amazon deserves a closer look.

On one side, the company loudly commits tens of billions to AI and automation. On the other, engineers who built core projects are let go with no warning—projects killed, teams dissolved, all explained away as “resource optimization.”

This is not desperate retrenchment in a downturn. Amazon has rolled out AI agents at scale to take over parts of traditional white-collar work: in analytics, AI can finish in minutes what used to take a team days; in project management and admin, AI can coordinate communication and run approvals.

The giants who shout that AI will liberate humanity are the first to cut the people who built the AI.

And there is a darker punch line.

The humans leave; their “digital twins” stay behind and keep generating value.

## What We Are Really Afraid Of

Every month brings a new buzzword; every few days another “game changer.” Under every headline, commenters tally how many jobs just died. No one knows which skill stays “safe” forever, yet someone always sells a course that promises to solve everything once and for all.

No one can live in that much noise.

Strip the anxiety down, though, and you get three fears: **falling behind**, **choosing wrong**, **missing the boat**. We mistake a long historical shift for a bet we have to place today.

That is a category error.

When the textile loom arrived, hand workers did not vanish overnight. Over decades they moved from hand to machine, from operators to people who ran and maintained the equipment.

It was a long, uneven co-evolution of people and machines.

The same pattern holds now—only faster.

Faster does not mean “you are obsolete tomorrow.”

**It does mean it is time to change.**

---

## The Hype Is Not Your Battlefield

In recent years, global AI startup funding has hit new highs, accounting for roughly half of all venture capital.

Some of the smartest people on Earth, backed by the densest pools of money, are solving models, compute, and infrastructure—the foundation layer.

After OpenClaw came ZeroClaw, QClaw, ArkClaw—a sprawl of “Claws” and an infrastructure arms race across the internet.

At the same time, the first wave of AI darlings is already failing. VCs say they only fund projects that can turn a profit within six months. How brutal that foundation war is, outsiders barely see.

Still—

that is not your battlefield.

You do not need to know how your refrigerator’s refrigeration cycle works. You care whether it cools well, whether it saves power, whether the materials are safe, whether it is easy to use. You need to know which shelf is for fresh food and which is for the freezer.

Your battlefield is the application layer.

Learn to use this “fridge” well first.

## A Range of Peaks, Not One Summit

Progress in the AI era is not a single highway; it is a mountain range—some peaks low and gentle, some high and steep.

Do not sprint for the highest peak on day one. That is how you fall hard.

The people who actually rewired their income were rarely the ones who read the most papers—they were the ones who wired AI into their workflows earliest. Some stop at a chat app; some are already on Claude Code; others are “changing the world.” Wherever you are, there is a path that fits.

---

### Peak One: Learn to Ask AI Well

This is the lowest climb. Most people try it a few times, shrug “that’s it,” and quit—because the bar feels so low they never take it seriously.

Throwing a vague sentence at AI versus designing a real question produces wildly different results. The point is not “learning the tool” but learning to untangle your own thinking and say it clearly. The clearer you are, the better the answer.

No idea where to start? Have AI interview you with Socratic prompts while you answer. That alone is a powerful pattern.

---

### Peak Two: Learn to Write Prompts

Rebuilding from scratch every time you face the same kind of problem is waste.

Prompts are how you freeze AI capability into something reusable—from basic prompts, to meta-prompts (AI helps you write better prompts), to prompt frameworks (AI fills in “parameters” and yields different outputs).

Many people miss this: writing a prompt is really stress-testing your own understanding of the task. The sharper your thinking, the better the prompt. Prompts force fuzziness into clarity—that is metacognition, and it is the price of entry to real expertise.

Past this point, you are already ahead of most people.

---

### Peak Three: Learn to Write Skills

Prompts answer “how do I ask each time?” Skills answer “what can *I* do?”

You have a repeatable way to build proposals.

You have a playbook for negotiations.

You have criteria for vetting partners.

That used to live only in your head—reloaded every time, exhausting, leaky, impossible to hand off.

Skills package that know-how so AI can call it like a library.

At this stage, you are ahead of the vast majority.

---

### Peak Four: Learn to Build Agents

An agent is a “digital employee” with memory, boundaries, and a defined remit.

I once helped a founder restructure this way. The team had been “manager gives orders, staff executes,” with information passed by people—error-prone and leaky. We introduced an agent team: each agent owned one slice of the business, did not share memory with the others, and passed only what was needed at handoff points.

The org went from “manager → individual contributors” to “manager → leads → agent teams.”

Flows sped up, data got finer-grained, and friction between people dropped. Critical SOPs were locked into the process instead of living in one person’s head. For the first time, the company relied on a system, not on heroes.

Multiple agents working together is where solo operators and “super individuals” actually win.

If you can build agents, you are past ninety-nine percent of people—and that is not hyperbole; it is the distribution today.

---

### Peak Five: Learn to Ship Productivity Tools

Here you start to **productize** capability.

You are not only using AI for yourself—you turn a process or capability into something others can use: an internal tool, an automation, a client-ready solution.

You do not have to be a programmer. You do need to know what problem the tool solves, who uses it, what changes after they use it, and where it can improve.

This is the line between “using AI” and “selling AI-powered capability.” You are no longer only a user—you are building tools.

Do not fear a sudden cliff in difficulty.

Reaching this peak is natural if you have done the work on peaks three and four.

Those layers are already your foundation.

---

By peak five, you are more than “fine” in the AI era.

Above that lies capital, teams, and messier market games—another league, and not something one essay can map. If you climb this far, those problems tend to find you.

---

## This Is Still Not a Gentle Story

Some people will be left behind.

Not by AI—by people who use AI well.

Those nearly hundred thousand roles at BYD were not “stolen” by models; they were quietly closed by a company that chose automation for work that no longer needed human repetition.

The mechanism differs from “AI took my job,” but the outcome is just as real.

MIT research from November 2025 estimates current AI can cover about 11.7% of U.S. labor tasks.

That share is still rising.

The pace of AI iteration really has outrun prior tech revolutions.

I wrote as much in that earlier post, and I still believe it.

The runway for adjustment is shorter than before.

The line between those who adapt and those who do not is simple: **whether you will take the first step.**

---

## No Need to Wander—Start Here

Find where you are on the ladder and move up one rung:

**Not using AI yet?**

Open any tool today and throw your most annoying task at it. Notice what changes.

**Using it, but casually?**

Pick something you repeat every week, write one solid prompt, save it, reuse it next time.

**Already have prompts?**

Turn your most important workflow into a Skill—not only what to do, but how and what “done” looks like.

**Already using Skills?**

Build your first agent: pick the role you most wish you could clone, and give it memory, capability, and rules.

**Already running agents?**

You probably know what comes next.

Do not try to climb all five peaks at once. Locate yourself, step up one level, and get stable before you go higher.

---

## Closing Thoughts

The OpenClaw hype is already cooling for most people.

The next wave will not wait long. A new model, a big launch, and the comments will explode again: “This time everything changes—what do I do?”

You do not need to prepay anxiety for the next cycle.

What you need is not to surf every wave, but to sync with the era’s rhythm and become **a native of the AI age**.

You stop fearing new labels because you are already using the tools. You stop fearing replacement because what you wield is **exactly what replaces others**.

Find your path.

Start today.
