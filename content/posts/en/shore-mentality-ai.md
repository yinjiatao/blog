Most people think they're "waiting for the right moment." They're not.

They're waiting for someone else to take the risk of that first step for them.

These are two completely different things.

## Stop Being the Person Standing on the Shore

In 1992, my father's colleague Old Zhang heard that someone had gone south to Shenzhen and earned a full year's salary in a single month.

He sat on a long bench in the factory locker room, clutching an enamel mug, listening to a visiting worker tell stories about the "Special Economic Zone." The stories were full of wealth, freedom, and a future simply unimaginable in the north.

He believed it. But not completely.

He told himself: "Let's wait and see. Wait until it's confirmed. Wait until the barrier is lower. Wait until someone shows the way."

Five years later, the factory was restructured. The iron rice bowl shattered. That "right moment" never came.

This isn't a story about nostalgia.

Is your attitude toward AI right now exactly like Old Zhang that afternoon, clutching his enamel mug?

> "I've heard about it, sounds interesting — but I'll wait and see."

I call this the **Shore Mentality**.

You can see the people in the water. You know the water isn't deep. But you just won't put your foot in.

Not because you can't swim. Because you've grown comfortable with the feeling of safety that comes from standing on the shore.

## The Three Voices That Keep You Stuck

The "Shore Mentality" isn't just an emotion. It's three overlapping thought patterns — like three gatekeepers blocking the path between you and action.

I've watched too many smart people get stopped by these three. They know AI exists. Some even open a chatbot every day to ask a question or two — and then nothing.

### Voice #1: "This is just another hype cycle."

The dot-com bubble. The blockchain revolution. The metaverse changing everything. Web3 disrupting it all. You've heard too many "this time it's different" slogans, and every time it's been the same — first the frenzy, then the bubble, then the silence.

So you've arrived at what seems like a very smart conclusion: skepticism equals rationality.

But here's the problem — you've confused "not getting played" with "not trying anything." Skepticism is right. But skepticism isn't an endpoint, it's a starting point. You should be skeptical, then go verify. Not skeptical, then sit down.

### Voice #2: "Learning this stuff is just too painful."

Reading documentation. Tuning parameters. Memorizing terminology. Figuring out what a token is, what temperature does, why the same question asked twice gives completely different answers?

Just thinking about it is exhausting.

You're already busy enough. Pushed around by your calendar every day — why take on definite pain for an uncertain reward?

This sounds reasonable, but it hides a fatal assumption: **you think "mastering AI" is the goal.**

It isn't. I'll get to that.

### Voice #3: "This isn't for someone like me."

You tried Claude Code, Cursor, Copilot — too technical. You tried ChatGPT and other chatbots — fine for conversation, but they can't help with your actual workflow. You're stuck in the middle: the deep stuff is out of reach, the shallow stuff is useless.

So you've given yourself a label: "I'm not a technical person."

Notice the structure of that sentence. It isn't describing your capability. It's defining your **identity**.

Once you accept the framing of "I'm not that kind of person," you never have to act again. Because inaction becomes "staying true to yourself."

**That's the most dangerous idea of all.**

## The Real Problem

By now, you might expect me to teach you how to write prompts, how to choose tools, how to build workflows.

I'm not going to.

Because most people's problems with AI aren't technical problems. They're **mindset problems**.

What do I mean?

Think about the people who went to Shenzhen in 1992. Did they all go to open factories and work in high-tech? No. Most of them just didn't want to follow the old rules anymore. They wanted a different way to live.

- **The old way:** Opportunities are assigned to you. Your job is to wait, obey, and not make mistakes.
- **The new way:** Opportunities are discovered through trying. Your job is to act, iterate, and validate quickly.

That shift in mindset matters a thousand times more than any specific skill.

The AI era is the same. You don't need to become a prompt engineer. You don't need to understand Transformer architecture. You don't need to spend hours on Hacker News pretending to be a tech insider.

What you need is a **mindset upgrade** — shifting from "I need to learn it first, then I can start" to "I'll start using it, and I'll learn naturally along the way."

This isn't wordplay. These are two completely different ways of living.

> From "learn before you use" to "learn by using."

## The 30-Minute Experiment

The method I use is called the **30-Minute Experiment**. It's simple — simple enough that you have no excuse not to try:

1. Set aside 30 minutes a day
2. Pick one specific, real problem from your life
3. Try to solve it with an AI tool
4. Regardless of the result

That's it. No "systematic learning path." No "beginner to expert." No courses, no certifications, no roadmap.

Why does this work? Because it bypasses those three voices that keep you stuck:

- It doesn't ask you to "believe" AI will change the world — you just need to try it once and see if it can help you draft this week's report;
- It doesn't ask you to invest huge amounts of time learning — 30 minutes is probably less than you spend scrolling through short videos every day;
- It doesn't ask you to become "a technical person" — you just need to sit down with a real problem.

**A three-week rhythm:**

**Week One: Solve a small task you've been putting off.** A first draft of a weekly report. A message you didn't know how to phrase. Run it through AI and see how far the output is from "usable."

**Week Two: Let AI handle a "structured" problem.** A pile of messy meeting notes. Research material that needs organizing. A chaotic project list. You provide the input; it provides the structure.

**Week Three: Try using AI for something you've always "wanted to do but couldn't figure out."** The outline for a project proposal. An article you've wanted to write but didn't know where to start. A plan you wanted to make but couldn't break into steps.

After three weeks, you won't be an AI expert. But you'll have something worth far more than an expert title:

**You'll have intuition.**

You'll instinctively know which problems are worth handing to AI and which aren't. That intuition can't be learned from any course. It can only grow from actual use.

## What This Column Is Really About

I'm writing this series with one goal: not to turn you into an AI expert.

Honestly, the label "AI expert" will probably be meaningless in two years — just like nobody calls themselves an "internet expert" today.

What I actually want to share is a set of **thinking tools** — to help you move from "I've heard of AI" to "I use AI naturally every day," completing that operating system upgrade.

In this series you'll find:

- **How to design your "first unavoidable use" scenario:** Not through willpower, but through environment design;
- **How to judge in 30 seconds whether a tool is worth 30 minutes of your time:** A minimal filtering framework;
- **How to find the leverage point in your workflow:** Not a total overhaul — just one point to move the whole chain.

None of this is high-barrier technical content. You don't need to know how to code. You don't need to spend money. You don't need more than 30 minutes a day.

You don't need to see the whole road before you take the first step. The only thing you need is to accept one premise:

> The change has already begun. And you can choose to be inside it, not outside it.
