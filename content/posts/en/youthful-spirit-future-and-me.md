For a while, I found it difficult to answer one of the most ordinary questions people asked me:

“What have you been up to lately?”

From having a job to being unemployed and staying at home, by any conventional measure, I could hardly call myself successful.

At least I used to have a job.

Every morning, I would get up, open my computer, work through requests, and wait for payday. Even if I had no idea where the future lay, I always knew what I was supposed to do that day.

After I lost my job, all of my time was suddenly handed back to me.

But that did not make me free.

On the contrary, when external structure disappears and a person has no inner direction, an abundance of time becomes a kind of pressure.

I knew time was passing, and I worried that the skills I had developed were losing value.

I felt an intense sense of urgency, yet I was so consumed by confusion and emptiness that I wanted to do nothing.

I called that state:

> **High awareness, low motivation.**

Older people might call it “having ambitions beyond my abilities.”

But I never quite agreed.

Sometimes, a person refuses to settle for just anything not because they look down on the opportunities in front of them, but because they have an inkling that what they spent years building should not be casually thrown away.

I knew I was not empty-handed, yet I could not say what exactly I had.

That was what failure most honestly felt like to me:

> Sitting on a mountain of treasure, with no idea how to mine it.

## I Had Always Been Fulfilling Other People’s Goals

When I worked for a company, I was responsible for only a tiny part of an enormous system.

The requirements had already been defined, and the direction had long since been decided. My job was simply to do my part well.

Later, I began taking on contract work.

On the surface, I had left the company and started working for myself.

In reality, I had merely left one fixed system and begun drifting between one client’s system and another.

The client made a request, and I implemented it.

The client changed their mind, and I kept revising.

Because I did not understand sales or know how to build a competitive advantage of my own, I was always compromising to accommodate users.

When I had a job, I fulfilled the company’s goals.

When I worked on contract, I fulfilled my clients’ goals.

I had always been developing products, yet I rarely created anything that was truly mine.

## Time Teaches Us to Compromise

A person does not suddenly lose their youthful spirit one day.

It fades slowly, one compromise at a time.

At first, you tell yourself:

> Just take care of what is in front of you.

Once you have made enough money, you can do what you truly love.

Later, you become accustomed to reshaping yourself around other people’s needs. Eventually, you stop even asking:

> What do I really want?

You are still busy, and you are still working hard.

But you have gone from being the protagonist of your own life to becoming a highly skilled supporting character.

When I speak of “resigning yourself to fate,” I do not mean that you stop working.

Quite the opposite: someone who has resigned themselves to fate may still work hard every day.

They simply no longer believe that they can truly choose a different life.

## A Daydream

When I first encountered AI, I asked it a question:

> “What kind of person can I become in the future?”

It did not answer directly.

Instead, it asked me:

> “What kind of person do you want to become?”

I thought for a moment and said something wildly unrealistic:

> I want to lie back while money keeps rolling in.

I thought it would laugh at me.

I thought it would tell me to stop dreaming, find a stable job, and earn money steadily, one step at a time.

But it did not.

It seriously discussed products, compounding, assets, and how an ordinary person could gradually gain greater freedom to choose.

Looking back, those answers were actually rather formulaic.

But at the time, I realized for the first time that even a seemingly absurd wish could be taken seriously.

It did not prove that I would succeed.

It simply made me realize:

> I do not have to accept the life I have now.

## Treating Myself as the Client for the First Time

In the past, I had always studied what other people needed.

What the company needed, what clients needed, what the market needed.

After that conversation, I began studying myself for the first time.

What kind of life did I want?

What was I genuinely good at?

What was I willing to keep creating over the long term?

Later, I began using AI to build products.

The first income I earned from a product of my own was not much, but it changed the way I understood making money.

In the past, whether I was employed or doing contract work, I had always been selling my time.

Products were different.

A piece of code could be reused. A method could be delivered again and again. One experience could become the starting point for the next action.

I began documenting what I had learned.

Later, as the amount of information grew, I had no choice but to build a knowledge base.

At first, it merely stored reference materials.

Gradually, it began storing my judgments, methods, failures, and even my debates with AI.

The knowledge base no longer merely answered the question, “What do I know?”

It began helping me decide:

> Given the problem at hand, what should I do?

And so the knowledge base became a decision-making system.

Later, it began taking part in execution as well.

Based on the materials I had collected and the judgments I had made in the past, it could propose different approaches, let multiple perspectives debate one another, and then generate content and products that could be tested quickly.

I called it:

> A second engine for my life.

It does not live my life for me.

It simply takes on the tedious, repetitive work that continually wears away at my energy.

I remain responsible for the most important things:

> Creation and decision-making.

## When the Speed of Execution Catches Up with the Speed of Thought

In the past, taking an idea from conception to validation might have required three days, a week, or even a month.

I had to collect information, devise an approach, compare different directions, and then develop the product.

Many ideas were never proven unworkable.

They simply never had the chance to be tested before my energy ran out.

After one project failed, it would take me a long time before I tried again.

Now, that process has become much faster.

I can now produce, in minutes, a first draft of a long-form essay that once took a week to write.

Product directions that once required endless deliberation can now be developed into several concrete approaches at once, allowing me to see what they really look like much sooner.

What changed me was not merely the speed.

It was that before failure had time to turn into self-doubt, I was already beginning the next round of brainstorming.

When the speed of execution finally caught up with the speed of thought, my youthful spirit slowly returned as well.

It did not make me blindly confident. Instead, it told me:

> If this does not work, there is still another possibility.

## I Started Out Wanting to Make Money in My Sleep. Now I Wake Up Eager to Work

It sounds a little contradictory.

I first became interested in AI because I wanted to “make money in my sleep.”

But now, when I wake up each day, one thought often runs through my mind:

“Time to get to work.”

Every day, I look forward to continuing to write, build products, and run new experiments.

But there is one fundamental difference between now and the past.

In the past, I worked because I had to.

Now, I work because I want to.

I spend my time doing things that interest me.

If something does not interest me, I can choose not to do it.

I can also stop at any time, step away, and return to life outside work.

Freedom, then, does not mean never working.

Freedom means having the right to stop whenever you choose.

What I truly wanted was never to lie around doing nothing.

I simply wanted survival to stop holding my time hostage, so that work could once again become an active choice.

## Letting the Past Settle into Something Solid

If someone asked me:

“What makes you say that you are no longer a failure?”

I could not prove it with money.

There will always be someone richer than me.

Nor could I prove it by how much free time I have.

There will always be someone freer than me.

All I can say is:

> I am content with my life now. I am excited about the future. And I am steadily moving toward the future I chose for myself.

That alone is something my former self did not have.

Even if AI disappeared one day, I would not become the person I used to be again.

Because what I have come to understand will not suddenly disappear.

My former self was like someone floating on the surface of the sea.

I was constantly buffeted by forces beyond my control: my industry, my work, my clients, and the times.

The sea still surrounds me now, and the broader forces around me remain beyond my control.

But the body of work, methods, and understanding I have accumulated are gradually becoming solid ground beneath my feet.

At last, I am no longer simply drifting with the current.

Step by step, I have begun walking toward a shore of my own.

Doing what I love.

Living freely.

## Look Ahead. Don’t Look Back.

I used to think that youthful spirit belonged to the young.

Later, I realized that it has nothing to do with age.

Youthful spirit is the willingness to keep looking forward to what lies ahead even after experiencing disappointment.

It is the willingness to begin again, even when you know the next attempt may also fail.

It is the moment when, one day, you finally stop asking:

“Can my life still change?”

And instead begin looking ahead with anticipation:

“What else can I do tomorrow?”

If I could return to that most bewildering period of my life and face the version of myself who had high awareness but low motivation, who had already begun to feel disillusioned with life, I would not tell him which tool was best.

Nor would I promise him that he would succeed.

I would only borrow a line from *The Long Season* and say to him:

> Look ahead. Don’t look back.
